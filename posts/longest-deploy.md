## The Schedule Nobody Talks About

Let me tell you what 120 hours in a pay period actually looks like.

24 on. 48 off. 24 on. 48 off. And then — because someone called out, because there's never enough people, because EMS has been hemorrhaging workers for years and nobody in charge seems to care — a mandatory recall. Another 24. Because the system needs you more than your family does.

That's not a schedule. That's a sentence.

And somewhere in those 24-hour shifts, you're supposed to sleep. You're allowed to — technically. When nothing's going on. When the tones aren't dropping. When the radio's quiet. When your brain finally stops replaying the last call long enough to close your eyes.

Good fucking luck.

So you run on nothing. You hold it together with caffeine and muscle memory and the same dark humor that gets every first responder through the unsurvivable. You go home. You try to be present. You fail more than you'd like to admit. You go back.

And you do it again.

## I'm Not Unemployed. I'm Trapped.

I want to be clear about something — because this isn't a story about a man who can't find work.

I have work. I have too much of it.

What I don't have is a life that looks anything like what I promised myself when I enrolled at SNHU, cracked open a laptop at 2 a.m. between shifts, and decided that a degree in computer science was going to be the bridge to something better. Something sustainable. Something that didn't consume every hour I had and then demand more.

Seven months ago I graduated. I did the thing. I earned it — not in a classroom with free time and a meal plan, but in stolen hours between shifts, with five daughters climbing on me and a brain that was simultaneously processing trauma calls and debugging C++.

And I'm still in the ambulance.

Not because I gave up. Not because I stopped trying. Because the door I built the key for hasn't opened yet.

## The Silence

Here's what nobody tells you about the tech job search: most companies won't even say no.

You spend hours tailoring a resume. You write a cover letter that's actually honest for once. You hit submit. And then — nothing. No acknowledgment. No rejection. No automated email. Just silence that stretches into days and then weeks until you're not even sure the application went anywhere at all.

A handful have sent the form email. *We've decided to move forward with other candidates.* And I mean this — that felt like a gift. At least it was something. At least some automated system somewhere confirmed I existed in their pipeline before discarding me.

The rest just ghost.

Some days that makes me angry. At the process. At whatever ATS system filtered me out before a human read a single word. At the idea that a man who made life-and-death decisions under fire, who earned a degree on no sleep, who built a portfolio from scratch — can't get a callback.

Some days it makes me doubt myself. Maybe the gap is wider than I think. Maybe I'm not as ready as I believe. Maybe this whole thing was a story I told myself to get through the hard years and the market just doesn't care about your story.

And some days I just keep going. Because I don't know how to quit. Because stopping isn't in the wiring.

But I won't lie and say it's fine.

## At What Cost

EMS saved me once. After the Army, after the deployments, after I came home with more weight than I could name — it gave me purpose when I had none. Trauma was familiar. The chaos kept the darker thoughts at bay. I needed it then.

But somewhere along the way the job stopped being something I chose and became something I was stuck in.

The work is real. Every call matters. I know that. I feel it every time. Life-and-death is not a metaphor in this job — it is the literal description of what's happening in the back of that rig.

And what does it pay? Pennies. Genuinely. The kind of wages that don't reflect the weight of being the last thing standing between someone and the worst day of their life. You carry trauma home for free. The overtime they demand from you when staffing falls apart — and it always falls apart — doesn't come close to compensating for what it costs on the other end.

But the money isn't even the sharpest edge of it.

It's not one missed moment. It's a pattern.

Christmas morning? Dad's on duty. Fourth of July — a holiday I hold close given everything I've given this country — Dad's on a rig, putting bandages on people who blew their hand off trying to enjoy the same fireworks his kids are watching without him. Birthdays. School events. The quiet Tuesday nights that don't seem important until you realize you've missed dozens of them.

The question my kids have learned to stop asking is "will Dad be home?" Because the answer is unpredictable enough that hope stopped being worth it.

"Saving lives" is meaningless to a child who just wants her father at the table.

Life doesn't stop. It keeps moving whether I'm there or not. And I keep missing it because the schedule demands it and the system doesn't care.

That's not a calling. That's consumption.

## Why I'm Leaving — And Why It's Hard

I'm not leaving EMS because I hate it. I'm leaving because I love my family more.

But I want to be honest about the guilt, because it's real and it doesn't go away just because the decision is right.

The people I work with are some of the most capable, resilient humans I've ever been around. You build a bond in this job that's hard to explain to someone who hasn't lived it — the kind forged in 3 a.m. calls and impossible situations and gallows humor that would horrify anyone outside the ambulance. Walking away from that carries weight.

And the patients are real. Every one of them. I don't take lightly the fact that some of them made it because I showed up. That matters. It always will.

But I've given enough. More than enough. I gave years of my body, my sleep, my holidays, my presence — to a system that will replace me without a second thought the moment I'm gone. The guilt of leaving belongs to me. The cost of staying belongs to my daughters.

I don't want to look back at their childhoods and see a man who was always on a shift. Always exhausted. Always half-present even when he was there.

I built this degree for them as much as for me. I proved I could do it under conditions most people wouldn't survive. I am ready.

Now I'm waiting for the market to believe that.

## Still Here

Seven months. Still applying. Still getting ghosted. Still running calls.

I'm frustrated. I doubt myself more than I'd like to admit. And I keep going anyway because the alternative is accepting that this is it — that the sacrifice doesn't pay off, that the silence wins.

I'm not there yet.

My family deserves a version of me that actually comes home. I'm not stopping until I get there.
